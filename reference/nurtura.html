Rather than adding more, it adds guardrails: letting users define what they're ready to see before they see anything.

THE BRIEF

Create a safe community platform for people navigating IVF

52% of IVF patients avoid online forums because the content is too triggering. Instead of adding more features to an already overwhelming landscape, I designed a community platform with emotional guardrails built in from onboarding.

CONTEXT

IVF is one of the most emotionally demanding medical experiences a person can go through

Existing platforms — Facebook groups, Reddit, generic forums — amplify negativity, lack content filtering, and offer no personalisation. The result: users leave more overwhelmed than when they arrived.

75% felt lonely during the process. 77% preferred a dedicated mobile app over web-based platforms. The market gap wasn't information — it was safe, filtered, community-based emotional support.

THE PROBLEM

People navigating IVF face significant emotional isolation

The specific failure mode of existing platforms is their inability to filter emotionally harmful content. Users encounter pregnancy announcements, success stories, and graphic medical content with no control over exposure — often at the worst possible moments.

USERS

Three people shaped the design

From 12 interviews and a 35-person survey, three personas drove primary decisions. Billie and Mintra defined the core product. Liam shaped the partner-facing onboarding path.

Billie — Emotional Rollercoaster

39, CEO · Gothenburg

Single mother, 3 years into IVF. Needs a safe space to express emotions without guilt when seeing others' successes.

Mintra — Overwhelmed

Wants a positivity-filtered community she can actually stay in without being derailed by others' announcements.

Liam — The partner

35, Civil Engineer · Stockholm

Supporting his wife through IVF. Struggles to find resources built for partners, not patients.

Billie and Mintra drove primary design decisions. Liam shaped the 'Supporting a partner' onboarding path and the For Partners section in the guide.

DESIGN DECISIONS

One screen that changes everything downstream

The standard solution to triggering content would be a settings screen. I put it in onboarding — before users ever see the feed.

KEY DECISION · S03

Content Preferences — why this screen exists

The screen asks: 'What would you like to avoid seeing?' — checkboxes for pregnancy announcements, success stories, medical procedures, baby/child images. Below: 'You can change this anytime.' That last line matters: it reduces commitment anxiety. The framing is 'you're in control' rather than 'we're protecting you.'

MORE

What I'd do differently

Three open questions left unresolved from v1:

How should the app handle a user who reports feeling unsafe or suicidal? A moderation and crisis resource flow is needed before this goes into production.

Should peer matches be algorithmically generated or user-browsable? Trade-off: algorithmic matching is more effective but removes the sense of agency that matters to this user group.

How should the app handle users who change journey stage mid-process? Stage-based personalisation breaks down when circumstances change unexpectedly.

Deliberately out of scope for v1: real-time chat, AI chatbot, clinic integration, Android. Each excluded to preserve focus on the core value proposition.
