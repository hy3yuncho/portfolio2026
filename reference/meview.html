THE BRIEF

Design a peer feedback app for Gen Z that responders will actually use

MeView lets users request structured peer feedback by project, receive it anonymously via a shareable link (no login required for responders), and view results as a visual skill map — organised by competency or project.

CONTEXT

A 3-month cross-functional project

Product and UI design was my full scope — research, IA, UI, and design system — working alongside a PM, graphic designer, and two developers. Hi-fi interactive prototype delivered in Figma, handed off to developers with component specs.

THE PROBLEM

Peer feedback has no structured home

Gen Z wants to understand how others perceive them, but peer feedback in practice is scattered, subjective, and hard to act on. Existing tools sit at two extremes — LinkedIn recommendations (too formal) or DMs and verbal comments (too informal). Nothing in between was designed for self-exploration.

Why it mattered: 80% of people are curious about how others see them, with Gen Z most engaged. The gap isn't lack of desire — it's structure.

The hardest constraint: getting people to actually respond. Asking for feedback is emotionally vulnerable — and on the responder side, following through is easy to deprioritise. The responder experience needed to be frictionless enough that people would actually complete it.

RESEARCH

The biggest barrier to feedback isn't giving it — it's asking for it

Methods: desk research + 10 semi-structured interviews (4 students, 6 job seekers). Interviews not surveys — we needed to understand the emotional context, not just the behaviour. Survey data wouldn't have surfaced why asking feels so vulnerable.

Key finding from desk research: post-pandemic rise in identity-focused behaviour. 80% of people curious about who they are — Gen Z most engaged.

Key finding from interviews: the biggest barrier to feedback isn't giving it. It's asking for it. Emotional vulnerability around requesting feedback was the core friction point.

DESIGN DECISIONS

Four decisions, each with a named trade-off

Every decision below had a real alternative. The trade-offs are included because they're part of the argument — not weaknesses, but honest constraints accepted in exchange for something more important.

1 — No-login responder flow

Situation Responders needed to leave feedback without friction. Requiring sign-up would cause significant drop-off — the request would arrive, feel like work, and get ignored.
Chosen No-login responder flow via shared link — open link, respond, done.
Trade-off Less accountability on the responder side. Potential for low-quality or insincere responses. Worth it: the volume increase from frictionless access outweighs the quality risk.

2 — Dual view: Skills and Projects

Situation Feedback could be organised by person, project, or skill. Needed to pick a primary structure without locking users into one goal.
Chosen Dual view — 'View by Skills' and 'View by Projects' — letting users switch based on their current goal.
Trade-off More complex IA. Required careful labelling to avoid confusion between the two modes.

3 — Anonymous by default

Situation Feedback on weaknesses is emotionally risky. Users might avoid requesting it, and responders might soften their answers to protect feelings.
Chosen Anonymous-by-default responses with optional written elaboration.
Trade-off Anonymity can reduce response accountability. The net effect is more honest, more frequent feedback — but outlier responses have no recourse.

4 — Bold, character-driven visual identity

Situation The brand needed to balance credibility with Gen Z appeal without feeling like a corporate HR tool.
Chosen Bold, kitsch visual identity with original mascot characters.
Trade-off Strong personality may not translate across cultures and could feel less 'professional' in some hiring contexts. A deliberate bet on the primary audience.

SOLUTION

Three flows: request, respond, review

Request flow — create a feedback form, choose project, select competency areas, share link via social or messenger. Responder flow — open link, select perceived strengths, optionally write elaboration. No login required. Results dashboard — view aggregated skill tags, switch between Skills and Projects views.

MORE

Reducing responder friction is the single biggest lever

The no-login flow wasn't just a nice-to-have — it was the core product decision. Everything else depended on people actually responding. This is the version of the insight that should have been the brief.

What I'd explore with more time: test the social sharing feature with real users to see whether output actually drives organic growth, or whether it's aspirational positioning. Consider a web version for accessibility across devices.
