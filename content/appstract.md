## Context

Appstract is a social shopping layer embedded as a widget into partner e-commerce sites — bringing communal discovery into online retail.

Solo designer, early-stage startup. End-to-end: product strategy and research through to high-fidelity UI. No other designers. I worked directly with founders on strategic direction and design decisions.

---

## Problem

The widget was live. Shoppers weren't noticing it.

Two sides to this:

**The shopper (B2C):** The widget's value proposition — shopping is better when shared — wasn't landing at the moment of use. The existing tooltip-based prompts assumed users would stop, read, and decide. On a product page, attention doesn't work that way.

**The partner site (B2B):** Even when the widget was present, it wasn't being actively surfaced or promoted. It was competing for attention it wasn't getting.

The core constraint: the widget lives inside someone else's site. Appstract doesn't control the surrounding page, layout, or context. Every solution had to earn attention without relying on the environment.

## Research

I ran user testing to understand where attention was dropping and what wasn't converting. I also did desk research into widget discovery patterns, contextual onboarding, and social proof mechanics in third-party embedded products.

Four principles shaped the design work:

1. **Earn attention before asking for action.** The widget needs to justify its presence before it asks for a click.
2. **Show the social value, don't describe it.** "Shopping is better when shared" needs to be visible — not explained.
3. **Design for the three-second window.** On a product page, that's the realistic time available.
4. **Be a good guest.** The widget lives on someone else's site — it should integrate without disrupting.

## Solution

The redesigned widget default state surfaces live social signals — peer activity, shared browsing, reactions — visible without any interaction required. The social activity creates curiosity before anyone has to click anything.

**Key flows designed:**

- **Widget default state** — ambient social signals visible from the moment the page loads
- **First-interaction moment** — frictionless entry from the product page into the shared shopping experience
- **Onboarding within the widget** — progressive disclosure, no page navigation required
- **Zero-state handling** — what the widget shows when social data is sparse, so early-stage low density doesn't undermine credibility
- **B2B placement guide** — recommended integration patterns for partner sites to maximise discoverability without disrupting the host page

## Impact

Qualitative testing confirmed users understood the widget's value faster with the ambient social signal approach than with the previous copy-led prompts. The redesign addressed the root cause — conceptual illegibility at the point of use — rather than iterating on the surface.

## Learnings

**Most important thing:** Awareness is a design problem, not a copy problem. The existing prompts failed not because the words were wrong, but because the interaction model assumed users would stop and read. Social signals create curiosity without requiring a conscious decision to engage.

**What I'd do differently:** Audit how partner sites were actually placing the widget earlier in the process. That would have shaped the design constraints more precisely from the start.

**What's next for v2:** Longitudinal testing to measure whether first-interaction users return and bring others. The real measure of the redesign isn't a single click — it's whether the social loop activates.
